EXPERIMENT 13 — ITALKI RECIPROCAL FALSIFICATION TEST

Purpose

Experiment 13 reverses the direction of the central claim by placing
Italki_Jew_(n=9) in the target position. Ashkenazi Jews are not allowed to
serve as a source.

The test therefore asks whether the central Mediterranean placement of an
Italian Jewish population appears independently of any Ashkenazi, Rhineland,
German, Polish, or Russian contribution.

Displayed result

Distance: 0.00984550

Retained sources:

Maltese_(n=8): 39.8
Italian_Calabria_Reggio_Calabria_(Calabrese)_(n=9): 28.8
Samaritan_(n=7): 23.4
Italian_Lombardy_(Lombard)_(n=10): 4.2
Italian_Sicily_West_(Sicilian)_(n=8): 2.0
Palestinian_Arab_Christian_(n=24): 1.8

Major finding 1: the Italki target resolves predominantly through central
Mediterranean sources

The strict central Mediterranean block is:

Maltese: 39.8
Calabria: 28.8
West Sicily: 2.0

Combined: 70.6

When Lombardy is added as the remaining Italian contribution, the total
assigned to Italian and central Mediterranean sources reaches
74.8.

The model therefore places nearly three quarters of the Italki profile on the
Italian and central Mediterranean side of the competition.

Major finding 2: Maltese and Calabria alone account for most of the model

Maltese and Calabria total:

39.8 + 28.8 = 68.6

These are the two largest retained sources. Their dominance is important
because the source panel also contains modern Levantine populations,
Northern/Central Italians, Aegean Greeks, Germans, Poles, and Russians.

The optimizer does not need Northern or Eastern Europeans to create the Italki
placement. It primarily selects Maltese and Calabrian structure.

Major finding 3: the Levantine-continuity signal is real but secondary

The retained Levantine-continuity block is:

Samaritan: 23.4
Palestinian Arab Christian: 1.8

Combined: 25.2

The strict central Mediterranean block of 70.6 is
approximately 2.80 times the retained Levantine-continuity block.

This supports a profile with substantial Levantine continuity embedded inside
a predominantly Italian and central Mediterranean structure. It does not
support describing the Italki target as a primarily Levantine population that
requires later Northern or Eastern European admixture to reach its present
position.

Major finding 4: Northern and Eastern European sources are not retained

German_(n=113): 0.0
Polish_(n=68): 0.0
Russian_Smolensk_(n=15): 0.0

All three Northern/Eastern European competitors are absent from the displayed
nonzero solution.

This is the core reciprocal falsification result. Italki Jews remained in
Italy and cannot owe their central Mediterranean placement to Ashkenazi
admixture acquired in the Rhineland or Eastern Europe. In this experiment,
that placement is reproduced independently through Maltese, Calabrian,
Sicilian, and Italian sources, together with a secondary Levantine-continuity
component.

Major finding 5: the result provides an independent control for the Ashkenazi
experiments

Experiments 10–12 show that Ashkenazi Jews are modeled most efficiently when
Italki and central Mediterranean sources are available, and that removing
those sources worsens the fit and inflates Northern Italian and Levantine
proxies.

Experiment 13 approaches the problem from the opposite direction. It tests the
Italian Jewish population itself without using Ashkenazi Jews as a source.

The Italki result independently reproduces the same broad structure:

1. a dominant central Mediterranean and Italian block;
2. a substantial but secondary Levantine-continuity block; and
3. no retained Northern or Eastern European source.

That convergence makes it difficult to explain the Ashkenazi–Italki affinity
as a product of later Rhineland or Eastern European history.

Major finding 6: Calabria is the dominant Southern Italian source

Calabria contributes 28.8, while West Sicily contributes 2.0. Apulia and
Campania receive zero in the displayed solution.

This does not show that Calabria was the only Southern Italian historical
source. The Southern Italian references are correlated and compete for the
same dimensions. In this panel, Calabria is the Southern Italian population
that most efficiently represents the relevant portion of the Italki target.

The key regional result is not that one modern province is uniquely ancestral.
It is that a Southern Italian source survives strongly and becomes the
second-largest coefficient in a broad competition.

Major finding 7: Maltese likely absorbs overlapping Sicilian, Southern
Italian, and eastern Mediterranean structure

Maltese is the largest source at 39.8, while West Sicily receives only 2.0 and
the Aegean sources receive zero.

Because Maltese, Sicilian, Southern Italian, and Aegean populations share
substantial central Mediterranean ancestry, the coefficients are not
independent historical percentages. Maltese can absorb dimensions that might
be distributed across several related populations in another panel.

The result should therefore be read regionally: the dominant signal belongs
to the central Mediterranean source space, not necessarily to one literal
modern Maltese ancestral population.

Major finding 8: Aegean zeros do not negate Greco-Roman affinity

Crete, Rhodes, and Mani receive zero in the displayed nonzero solution.
These zeros occur while genetically overlapping Maltese and Calabrian sources
receive 68.6 combined.

In a constrained competition model, correlated sources can substitute for one
another. The Aegean zeros mean that Maltese and Calabria represent the target
more efficiently in this exact panel. They do not establish the historical
absence of Aegean or Greco-Roman ancestry.

Major finding 9: the small Lombardy coefficient does not make the profile
Northern Italian

Lombardy contributes only 4.2. Tuscany receives zero.

This is sharply different from Experiment 12, where Lombardy inflated to 38.0
only after Italki and the broader central Mediterranean source space were
removed. With appropriate Southern Italian, Sicilian, Maltese, and Aegean
comparators restored in Experiment 13, Lombardy falls to a minor role.

This contrast reinforces the source-omission argument: Northern Italian
coefficients can become artificially large when the relevant central
Mediterranean comparators are unavailable.

Conclusion

Experiment 13 is a strong reciprocal falsification test.

The Italki Jewish target is modeled as:

70.6 strict central Mediterranean;
74.8 Italian plus central Mediterranean when Lombardy is
included;
25.2 Levantine continuity; and
0.0 Northern/Eastern European in the displayed solution.

The result demonstrates that the central Mediterranean placement shared by
Italki and Ashkenazi Jews exists independently in an Italian Jewish population
that did not require Rhineland or Eastern European admixture to acquire it.

Within this model, the most economical interpretation is an Italian Jewish and
central Mediterranean foundation with a substantial secondary
Levantine-continuity component.

Methodological caution

The displayed values are constrained similarity coefficients, not exact
historical ancestry percentages. Modern populations are proxies, and closely
related Southern Italian, Sicilian, Maltese, and Aegean sources can replace one
another.

The strongest inference is the reciprocal design itself: Ashkenazi Jews are
excluded as a source, Northern/Eastern European populations are allowed to
compete, none is retained, and the Italki target independently resolves
primarily through Italian and central Mediterranean source space.
