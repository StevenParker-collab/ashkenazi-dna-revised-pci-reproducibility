EXPERIMENT 15 — IRON AGE SOUTHERN LEVANT CONTINUITY MODEL

Purpose

Three Iron Age coastal southern-Levantine population averages are modeled
against pre-Iron-Age Levantine site-period averages together with transparent
Mycenaean and Anatolian Bronze Age composite averages.

The composites are unweighted means of all predeclared qualifying site
averages. They are population-level composites, not selected individuals.

The experiment is a validation test. It asks whether the same constrained
framework used elsewhere in the repository can recover strong Bronze Age
southern-Levantine continuity when genuine Iron Age southern-Levantine targets
are supplied.

Major finding 1: the model recovers overwhelming Bronze Age southern-Levantine
continuity

Across the three Iron Age targets, the average assigned to pre-Iron-Age
southern-Levantine site-period sources is 89.8.

The target-specific continuity totals are:

Philistine Ashkelon: 100.0
Phoenician-Achaemenid Achziv: 95.4
Iron Age Beirut: 74.0

This is the central validation result. When actual Iron Age southern-Levantine
population averages are modeled, the framework overwhelmingly returns earlier
southern-Levantine populations.

Major finding 2: the Philistine Ashkelon target is modeled entirely through
earlier Levantine sources in this panel

Israel_IAII_Philistine_Ashkelon_(n=3) receives:

Jordan_LBA_Baq'ah_(n=8): 52.4
Lebanon_MBA_Sidon_Canaanite_(n=5): 22.8
Israel_LBA_Megiddo_(n=8): 18.2
Israel_MBA_Megiddo_(n=7): 6.6

Combined: 100.0

Both the Mycenaean and Anatolian composites are 0.0.

This result is particularly important because the target label is Philistine.
The calculator does not mechanically assign a Mycenaean coefficient merely
because a historically Aegean-associated label is present. It models the
population average according to its full coordinate structure.

Major finding 3: Achziv is also overwhelmingly continuous with the Bronze Age
Levant

Israel_IAII_Phoenician-Achaemenid_Achziv_(n=4) receives:

Lebanon_MBA_Sidon_Canaanite_(n=5): 35.8
Israel_LBA_Megiddo_(n=8): 30.2
Israel_MBA_Megiddo_(n=7): 29.4
Mycenaean composite: 4.6

The pre-Iron-Age southern-Levantine total is 95.4.

The Mycenaean coefficient is small and secondary. The result therefore
identifies very strong local continuity rather than a predominantly external
Aegean replacement.

Major finding 4: Beirut retains a strong Levantine majority while showing the
largest external composite signal

Lebanon_IAIII_Beirut_(n=8) receives:

Lebanon_MBA_Sidon_Canaanite_(n=5): 57.4

Israel_MBA_Megiddo_(n=7): 0.6

The pre-Iron-Age southern-Levantine total is 74.0.
The combined Mycenaean and Anatolian composite total is 26.0.

Beirut therefore shows the greatest external eastern Mediterranean shift in
the three-target panel, but the dominant structure remains local Levantine
continuity.

Major finding 5: Sidon Canaanite is the most persistent and largest average
source

Lebanon_MBA_Sidon_Canaanite_(n=5) is retained in all three targets:

Philistine Ashkelon: 22.8
Achziv: 35.8
Beirut: 57.4

Displayed average: 38.7

It is the largest average individual source in the experiment. Its progressive
increase from Ashkelon to Achziv to Beirut also follows the northern coastal
geography of the targets, although this geographical pattern should be treated
as descriptive rather than as a formal test.

Major finding 6: local Levantine source allocation varies by target while
regional continuity remains stable

Philistine Ashkelon is anchored most strongly by Jordan_LBA_Baq'ah at 52.4.

Achziv distributes its continuity across Sidon, LBA Megiddo, and MBA Megiddo.

Beirut is anchored primarily by Sidon at 57.4.

The exact source coefficients change, but the regional category does not:
all three targets remain primarily or entirely pre-Iron-Age southern
Levantine.

This demonstrates why category-level continuity is more informative than
treating one archaeological site average as a literal historical ancestor.

Major finding 7: the Mycenaean and Anatolian composites behave selectively,
not indiscriminately

The Mycenaean composite is:

0.0 in Philistine Ashkelon
4.6 in Achziv
17.2 in Beirut

The Anatolian EBA composite is:

0.0 in Philistine Ashkelon
0.0 in Achziv
8.8 in Beirut

The composites are therefore not being forced into every target. They appear
only where they improve the fit and remain secondary to Levantine continuity.

This selectivity supports the use of the panel as a methodological control.

Major finding 8: fit quality varies across the three targets

Distances are:

Beirut: 0.01231731
Achziv: 0.01754960
Philistine Ashkelon: 0.02428549

Beirut has the closest fit and Philistine Ashkelon the weakest fit.

The weaker Ashkelon fit means the 100.0 Levantine allocation should not be read
as proof that no ancestry associated with Aegean migration ever existed.
Rather, within this exact population-average panel, the target is represented
most economically by the available Bronze Age Levantine sources.

Major finding 9: the experiment validates the framework before later Jewish
targets are interpreted

The repository argues that source choice matters. Experiment 15 demonstrates
that the calculator can recover strong local continuity when the appropriate
earlier regional references are available.

For genuine Iron Age southern-Levantine targets, the model returns:

89.8 average Bronze Age southern-Levantine continuity;
7.3 average Mycenaean composite;
2.9 average Anatolian EBA composite.

The method therefore does not automatically manufacture a central
Mediterranean result for every eastern Mediterranean population. It recognizes
southern-Levantine continuity when that is the dominant structure.

Major finding 10: Experiment 15 strengthens the interpretation of the Jewish
comparisons by providing a positive continuity control

In earlier experiments, Italki and Ashkenazi populations do not reproduce the
same structure as the ancient Levantine targets. Experiment 15 shows what a
clear continuity result looks like under the same general modeling framework:
the earlier regional sources dominate, external composites remain secondary,
and the continuity pattern is visible across multiple targets.

That contrast supports the interpretation that the central Mediterranean
signals observed in Italki and Ashkenazi Jews are not simply artifacts of the
calculator's inability to recognize Levantine continuity.

Conclusion

Experiment 15 successfully recovers Bronze Age southern-Levantine continuity
in all three Iron Age coastal targets.

The unweighted average model is:

89.8 pre-Iron-Age southern Levant;
7.3 Mycenaean composite;
2.9 Anatolian EBA composite.

Philistine Ashkelon is modeled as 100.0 earlier Levantine in this panel.
Achziv is 95.4 earlier Levantine.
Beirut is 74.0 earlier Levantine with a 26.0 external eastern Mediterranean
composite signal.

The experiment therefore functions as a strong positive control: the framework
can identify local southern-Levantine continuity when it is present.

Methodological caution

These are constrained similarity coefficients, not exact historical ancestry
percentages. Archaeological population averages and composite averages are
proxies, and correlated Levantine sources can substitute for one another.

The Mycenaean and Anatolian composites are unweighted averages of qualifying
site averages. Their coefficients should not be interpreted as direct
genealogical percentages from one named civilization.

The strongest inference is the category-level result across all three targets:
pre-Iron-Age southern-Levantine sources overwhelmingly dominate the models.
